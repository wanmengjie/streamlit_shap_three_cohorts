# Development and validation of a prediction model for incident depression–cognition comorbidity in older adults in China: a CHARLS-based study with exploration of potential intervention effects

**完整版 | 基于 2026-03-20 运行结果**

---

## Abstract

**Background.** Depression–cognition comorbidity in older adults is a powerful predictor of dementia and accelerated functional decline. Identifying high-risk subgroups and quantifying causal effects of modifiable lifestyle factors remain methodologically challenging. We aimed to develop a stratified framework integrating predictive modeling and causal inference to address this gap.

**Methods.** Using longitudinal data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), we enrolled 14,386 individuals aged ≥60 years and divided them into three baseline cohorts: healthy (Cohort A, n=8,828), depression-only (Cohort B, n=3,123), and cognition-impaired-only (Cohort C, n=2,435). Comorbidity risk prediction (14 machine learning models, 5-fold grouped cross-validation), SHAP interpretability analysis, and XLearner causal estimation were performed for each cohort. PSM and PSW were used to cross-validate causal effects.

**Results.** Incidence of comorbidity varied distinctively: 4.1% (Cohort A), 13.6% (Cohort B), and 16.9% (Cohort C). Prediction models achieved moderate-to-good discrimination, with CPM champion AUCs of **0.74** (Cohort A, **MLP**), **0.67** (Cohort B, **ExtraTrees**), and **0.66** (Cohort C, **logistic regression**) (`Cohort_*/01_prediction/table2_*_main_performance.csv`). XLearner causal analysis revealed that regular exercise significantly reduced incident comorbidity risk in Cohort B (ATE = −0.042, 95% CI: −0.079 to −0.009, bootstrap *P* ≈ 0.018), with overlap assumption satisfied, moderate covariate balance (**max SMD ≈ 0.23**), and E-value **≈ 2.0** (conservative **≈ 1.34**). No significant effect was observed in Cohort A or C for exercise. In Cohort A, current drinking was associated with lower risk (ATE = −0.006, 95% CI: −0.017 to <0), and normal BMI with higher risk (ATE = 0.014, 95% CI: 0.001–0.077). In Cohort C, low chronic disease burden was associated with increased comorbidity risk (ATE = 0.063, 95% CI: 0.015 to 0.108), possibly reflecting reverse causality. Cross-validation with PSM and PSW showed directionally consistent effects for exercise in Cohort B. External validation yielded AUCs of 0.57–0.70 across cohorts. Decision Curve Analysis confirmed clinical net benefit at threshold probabilities of 5%–35%.

**Conclusions.** A three-cohort framework enables stratified prediction and causal assessment of incident depression–cognition comorbidity. Regular exercise may reduce comorbidity risk in older adults with depression but not in those with cognitive impairment alone, where reverse causality may apply. The integrated approach can inform precision geriatrics and targeted lifestyle interventions.

**Keywords:** Depression–cognition comorbidity; Causal machine learning; CHARLS; XLearner; SHAP; Predictive modeling

## 1 Introduction

The global aging demographic faces an escalating crisis of neurodegenerative and neuropsychiatric disorders. Among these, depression-cognition comorbidity is a powerful predictor of dementia, synergistically accelerating the loss of independence and elevating mortality rates [1]. In China, the burden is substantial; recent estimates suggest the prevalence of cognitive impairment among older adults to be as high as 44.04% [2], frequently compounded by depressive symptoms [3]. Accurate identification of risk factors and causal mechanisms is necessary to inform preventative strategies and clinical interventions in aging populations.

Despite this recognized epidemiological link, clinical management remains hampered by the heterogeneity of disease trajectories. Traditional epidemiological studies typically lump all older adults into a single group, applying standard regression models that estimate average population effects. This approach obscures critical sub-population dynamics, making it impossible to determine *when* and *for whom* specific lifestyle interventions are most effective.

Furthermore, identifying actionable therapeutic targets requires moving beyond mere prediction to robust causal inference. While traditional machine learning excels at predicting risk, it does not inform intervention. Conversely, randomized controlled trials (RCTs) for long-term lifestyle interventions in frail older adults are often logistically and ethically prohibitive. Recent breakthroughs in causal machine learning (CML)—such as meta-learners (e.g., XLearner) and Double Machine Learning—offer a framework to estimate heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE) from observational data [4,5]. CML bridges the gap between predictive accuracy and causal discovery by rigorously adjusting for high-dimensional confounders.

To address these methodological and clinical challenges, we developed a **"Three-Cohort Causal Machine Learning Framework"** using longitudinal data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS). By stratifying older adults into distinct baseline states, we aimed to: (1) identify high-risk subgroups and cohort-specific predictive signatures; (2) quantify causal effects rather than mere associations for modifiable lifestyle factors using the XLearner algorithm; and (3) define an actionable window for prevention. Ultimately, this study seeks to provide a translatable blueprint for precision geriatrics, pinpointing the exact clinical window where targeted lifestyle modifications can disrupt the progression of cognitive-affective decline.

## 2 Methods

### 2.1 Study Design
Incident cohorts were defined as individuals with wave-specific baseline records who had no depression–cognition comorbidity (i.e., depression and cognitive impairment did not coexist) and no prior history of such comorbidity in all preceding CHARLS waves. Depression was defined as a CES-D-10 score ≥10, and cognitive impairment was defined as a total cognitive score ≤10. The baseline population was divided into three cohorts: Cohort A (healthy), consisting of individuals with neither depression nor cognitive impairment at baseline; Cohort B (depression-only), comprising those positive for depression but with normal cognition at baseline; and Cohort C (cognition-impaired-only), including individuals with cognitive impairment but negative for depression at baseline. The primary outcome was the occurrence of depression–cognition comorbidity in the immediate next wave. The interval between CHARLS waves is 2 years, so the follow-up duration was 2 years (between adjacent waves).

Study data were derived from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationwide, prospective longitudinal cohort study employing a multistage stratified probability sampling method, covering middle-aged and elderly individuals in 28 provinces of China. CHARLS waves 1–4 (corresponding to survey years 2011, 2013, 2015, 2018) were used. Each record represents a person-wave: exposure and covariates were taken from Wave(*t*), and the outcome (incident comorbidity) from Wave(*t*+1); only records with a valid next-wave observation (i.e., *next_wave_val* = *wave* + 1) were retained. Prior comorbidity was defined as any preceding wave in which the individual met both CES-D≥10 and cognitive score≤10. The survey includes multiple dimensions such as demographic characteristics, physical health, mental health, lifestyle, and socioeconomic status. This study extracted complete information of individuals aged 60 years and above from each wave of CHARLS, followed by data cleaning, missing value handling (multiple imputation for general variables; complete case analysis for key variables, details in **Supplementary Text S1**), and cohort division.

### 2.1.1 Sample Size
The final sample size (*n* = 14,386 person-waves) was determined by applying eligibility criteria to the CHARLS waves 1–4 cohort (age ≥60, incident design, no prior comorbidity, valid next-wave outcome). No formal power calculation was performed a priori; the study was designed as an exploratory analysis of an existing longitudinal survey. Post hoc, for a binary outcome with ~5–17% incidence across cohorts and 14 candidate models, the 80:20 train–test split provided adequate events for bootstrap CI estimation (e.g., Cohort B *n* ≈ 2,500 in test fold with ~340 events).

### 2.1.2 Definition of Modifiable Factors
We defined five key modifiable factors based on standard criteria and questionnaire responses: regular exercise (moderate-intensity physical activity at least once a week), drinking (current drinking), social isolation, normal BMI (18.5–24 kg/m²), and low chronic disease burden (≤1 physician-diagnosed condition) (**Supplementary Table S3**). These definitions were consistent across all waves and cohorts.

### 2.2 Predictive Modeling
Five-fold grouped cross-validation was adopted, with GroupKFold used to divide the training set and test set (80%:20%) **grouped by individual ID**, avoiding the same patient appearing in both sets across waves. Missing covariates were handled using multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE; **Supplementary Text S1**). The imputation variable set included continuous covariates (age, BMI, waist, blood pressure, pulse, grip, gait speed, ADL/IADL, family size, income, sleep) and categorical covariates (gender, chronic diseases, residence, education, marital status, exercise, drinking, social isolation, pension, insurance, retirement). Continuous variables were imputed using the method with lowest NRMSE among six candidates (mean, median, linear regression, Bayesian ridge, KNN, MissForest); ordinal variables (education, self-rated health) via Bayesian regression; binary variables via random forest classifier; nominal variables via mode. **Imputation flow:** (1) single imputation with each of six methods; (2) NRMSE-based selection of the best method; (3) the selected method was run five times to generate five imputed datasets (m1–m5); (4) Rubin's rules were applied to pool AUC and ATE estimates across imputations for the primary analysis. SHAP and interpretability figures used a single imputed dataset for consistency. **Leakage control (as implemented):** Primary covariate imputation (MICE pipeline → analysis CSV) uses the **entire eligible analytic sample before** the 80:20 train–test split **grouped by individual ID**; it is **not** restricted to training folds only. **Machine-learning Pipelines** (residual imputation/scaling, if any) are **fit on training data only** within cross-validation, and CPM thresholds are **not** tuned on the held-out test set (TRIPOD). (**Supplementary Texts S1, Table S1, Figures S1, S4**). Hyperparameter random search was performed for 14 machine learning models (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost, GBDT, ExtraTrees, AdaBoost, Decision Tree, Bagging, KNN, MLP, Naive Bayes, SVM, HistGBM, and LightGBM; **Supplementary Text S2**).

The champion model was selected by **CPM (Comprehensive Performance Metric)**, a two-step selection procedure (not a weighted composite formula). **Step 1:** For each candidate model *m*, the optimal decision threshold τ_m maximizing the Youden index (Youden = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1) was determined strictly on the internal validation set (80% of the training fold via out-of-fold predictions), avoiding test-set optimization per TRIPOD guidelines [14]. **Step 2:** Each model was evaluated on the held-out validation set (20% of the training fold) at its own τ_m; the model with the highest AUC at that threshold was selected as the champion. **Operational definition:** champion = argmax_m AUC_m(τ_m), where τ_m = argmax_τ [Sensitivity_m(τ) + Specificity_m(τ) − 1] on the validation split. In code: rank all models by AUC (descending) at their respective Youden-optimal thresholds; the top-ranked model is the champion. Predictive performance was evaluated using AUC, AUPRC, Recall, Specificity, Youden index, Brier score, and 95% confidence intervals via stratified bootstrap (1,000 resamples).

### 2.3 Interpretability Analysis
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) was used for feature attribution of the champion model. The ranking of modifiable factors in the prediction serves as a preliminary validation for subsequent causal inference. This study focused on analyzing the SHAP mean values and marginal effect distributions of modifiable factors across cohorts.

### 2.4 Causal Inference Framework and Identification Strategy
We adopted the Potential Outcomes Framework and employed **XLearner** (Künzel et al.) as the primary causal estimator, which typically outperforms TLearner when treatment/control groups are imbalanced. Our identification strategy relies on four assumptions: 
1. **Unconfoundedness:** no unmeasured confounding given covariates.
2. **Positivity (Overlap):** verified by trimming samples with propensity scores outside [0.05, 0.95].
3. **Consistency:** observed outcome equals potential outcome under received treatment.
4. **No Interference (SUTVA):** one individual's treatment assignment does not affect another's outcome.

We assessed overlap via propensity score distribution and trimming proportion; covariate balance via standardized mean difference (SMD), with |SMD|<0.1 indicating good balance; and sensitivity to unmeasured confounding via E-value (VanderWeele & Ding, 2017).

### 2.5 Causal Estimation
XLearner was fitted with Random Forest nuisance models (μ₀, μ₁, π) using fixed hyperparameters (*n*_estimators=200, max_depth=4, min_samples_leaf=15) chosen to limit overfitting with high-dimensional covariates; no grid search was performed. Overlap trimming was applied: samples with propensity score outside [0.05, 0.95] were excluded before estimation. ATE and 95% confidence intervals were estimated via 200 non-stratified bootstrap resamples (with replacement on the full sample, refitting XLearner each time) when closed-form intervals were unavailable. Five interventions were evaluated: exercise, drinking, social isolation, normal BMI, and low chronic disease burden.

### 2.6 Cross-Validation of Causal Effects
Propensity Score Matching (PSM, 1:1 nearest neighbor) used a caliper of 0.024×SD of the propensity score [15]; sensitivity analyses with 0.1×SD and 0.3×SD showed minimal impact on covariate balance. Propensity Score Weighting (PSW) used inverse probability weighting with weight trimming to [0.1, 50] and propensity scores restricted to [0.01, 0.99], following Sturmer et al. [16] to reduce bias from extreme weights. PSM post-matching max SMD and PSW weighted SMD were reported; covariate-level SMD distributions are available in the reproducibility output (`assumption_balance_smd_*.csv`).

### 2.7 Sensitivity Analysis
Cutoff value sensitivity analysis was conducted (**Supplementary Table S2**): depression defined as CES-D≥8, ≥10, ≥12; cognitive impairment as score ≤8, ≤10, ≤12. Placebo test (100 resamples) and E-value analysis were performed for unmeasured confounding sensitivity. In addition, a bias-sensitivity simulation was conducted: we simulated an unmeasured confounder with varying strength of association (RR) with both exposure and outcome, and examined how the estimated ATE changed (**Supplementary Figure S14**, `fig_bias_sensitivity.png`). Feature noise stability was evaluated.

### 2.8 External Model Validation
Temporal validation (train wave<4, validate wave=4) and regional validation (train East+Central, validate West) were conducted. AUC, AUPRC, Brier score, and calibration curves were used as core indicators.

### 2.9 Calibration and Clinical Decision Curve Analysis
Calibration curves were plotted, and calibration slopes were calculated (**Supplementary Figure S5**). Brier decomposition was performed. Decision Curve Analysis (DCA) was conducted to determine the optimal threshold for clinical screening.

### 2.10 Unmeasured Confounding Bias Analysis
E-value was calculated for each intervention, quantifying the intensity of unmeasured confounding required to reverse the causal conclusion (VanderWeele & Ding, 2017).

### 2.11 Ethics and Data Availability
The data were derived from CHARLS. The original study was approved by the Ethical Review Committee of Peking University (IRB00001052-11015). Data are available at http://charls.pku.edu.cn/.

## 3 Results

### 3.1 Study Population
After applying inclusion and exclusion criteria (Figure 1), the incident cohort comprised 14,386 person-waves. Sample flow: 96,628 raw records → 49,015 (age ≥60) → 43,048 (CES-D non-missing) → 31,574 (cognition non-missing) → 16,983 (next-wave comorbidity non-missing) → 14,386 incident cohort. Baseline characteristics by cohort are shown in Table 1.

### 3.2 Incidence of Comorbidity
Incidence was highest in Cohort C (16.9%), followed by Cohort B (13.6%) and Cohort A (4.1%) (P<0.001).

### 3.3 Prediction Performance
Champion models (selected by CPM) achieved AUCs of **0.74** (Cohort A, **MLP**), **0.67** (Cohort B, **ExtraTrees**), and **0.66** (Cohort C, **logistic regression**) (Table 2; `Cohort_*/01_prediction/table2_*_main_performance.csv`). Approximate Recall/Specificity at Youden-optimal thresholds: **0.71/0.60** (A), **0.52/0.71** (B), **0.71/0.52** (C). SHAP summary plots for the champion models are shown in **Figure 3** (main-text ROC figure omitted; full model comparison in Table 2).

### 3.4 Causal Effect of Exercise
XLearner estimated the ATE of exercise on incident comorbidity. In **Cohort B (Depression-only)**, exercise significantly reduced comorbidity risk (ATE = −0.042, 95% CI: −0.079 to −0.009, bootstrap *P* ≈ 0.018). In Cohort A (Healthy), the effect was negligible (ATE = −0.003, 95% CI: −0.014 to 0.011). In Cohort C (Cognition-impaired-only), the point estimate was positive but non-significant (ATE = 0.020, 95% CI: −0.003 to 0.100), suggesting possible reverse causality.

### 3.5 Causal Validation: Overlap, Balance, and Robustness to Unmeasured Confounding
For Cohort B (exercise), overlap was satisfied after trimming (<1% of retained sample had PS outside [0.05, 0.95]); **~50%** of the original cohort was trimmed. Covariate balance was moderate (**max SMD ≈ 0.23**, **6** covariates with |SMD|≥0.1). E-value was **~2.0** (point) and **~1.34** (conservative). For Cohort A, max SMD was **~0.20**. For Cohort C, overlap was violated (**~18%** outside [0.05, 0.95]) and max SMD was **~0.86**, limiting causal interpretability (Table 4, **Supplementary Figures S2, S3**).

### 3.6 Other Modifiable Factors
Beyond exercise, **two contrasts had 95% CIs excluding zero in the primary XLearner summary** (`table4_ate_summary.csv`): **current drinking** in Cohort A (ATE = −0.006, 95% CI: −0.017 to ~0) and **normal BMI** in Cohort A (ATE = 0.014, 95% CI: 0.001–0.077). These associations may reflect residual confounding or selection and should not be interpreted as causal without further design. **Low chronic disease burden** was associated with **increased** comorbidity risk in Cohort C (ATE = 0.063, 95% CI: 0.015 to 0.108, bootstrap *P* ≈ 0.008), possibly reflecting reverse causation. Social isolation and the remaining cells in Table 5 were not significant at the 95% CI level (see **Figure 4A** for forest plot).

### 3.7 Subgroup Heterogeneity
In Cohort B, exercise-related CATEs are shown in Table 3 and **Figure 4B** (`table3_subgroup_cohortB.csv`；旧名 `table3_subgroup_axisB.csv` 与 consolidate 双写内容相同). Across all evaluated strata, point estimates ranged from about **−0.055 to −0.014**; **most larger subgroups** (e.g. residence, age, sex, chronic burden) clustered near **−0.038 to −0.047**. One self-rated health stratum (SRH_1, *n* = 111, pipeline flag: underpowered) showed a smaller magnitude (~−0.014). Formal subgroup×treatment interaction tests (e.g., regression with interaction terms) were not performed; CATEs are exploratory. *Exercise dose–response* (frequency/duration vs. comorbidity risk) was not analyzed because the primary exposure was binary (≥1×/week vs. <1×/week); exploratory dose–response for continuous exposures (e.g., sleep hours) is available via `charls_dose_response.py`. Interpretation should weigh cell size and the exploratory nature of subgroup effects.

### 3.8 Cross-Validation with PSM and PSW
For exercise in Cohort B, PSM yielded ATE = −1.6% (95% CI: −3.9% to 0.7%, non-significant), PSW yielded ATE = −2.8% (95% CI: −5.3% to −0.4%, significant), and XLearner yielded ATE = −4.2% (95% CI: −7.9% to −0.9%, significant). All three methods showed a protective direction; XLearner produced the largest effect (**Supplementary Table S5**).

### 3.9 External Validation
Temporal validation: AUC **0.68** (A), **0.57** (B), **0.57** (C). Regional validation: AUC **0.70** (A), **0.65** (B), **0.58** (C), rounded from `table6_external_validation_cohort*.csv`（旧名 `table6_external_validation_axis*.csv` 等同）. AUPRC and Brier scores are in **Supplementary Table S4**; 95% CIs and calibration curves for external validation may be added in future analyses. Cohort A showed the best generalizability; formal statistical tests for temporal vs. regional AUC differences were not performed.

### 3.10 Clinical Decision Support
DCA indicated net benefit at threshold probabilities of approximately 5%–35% (Figure 5; calibration, **Supplementary Figure S5**). Calibration slopes from `Cohort_*/04_eval/calibration_brier_report.txt` were **~4.42** (Cohort B) and **~1.02** (Cohort C). Counterfactual simulations for high-risk individuals in Cohort B showed that hypothetical adoption of regular exercise could reduce predicted 2-year comorbidity risk.

## 4 Discussion

### 4.1 Principal Findings
To our knowledge, this is the first study to disentangle heterogeneous trajectories of late-life depression-cognition comorbidity using a predictive-causal hybrid machine learning framework in a nationally representative longitudinal cohort. Incidence varied substantially by baseline status (4.1% in healthy, 13.6% in depression only, 16.9% in cognitive impairment only). Our principal finding is the identification of a highly specific therapeutic window: regular exercise significantly and causally reduces the risk of developing comorbidity, but *only* for individuals who already exhibit depressive symptoms without cognitive impairment (ATE = −0.042). This precision-medicine insight challenges the conventional "one-size-fits-all" approach to lifestyle interventions in aging, offering a highly targeted strategy for clinical practice.

The differential efficacy of exercise across the three cohorts carries profound clinical implications. In the healthy cohort (Cohort A), the baseline risk of transitioning to full comorbidity within two years is low (4.1%). Here, a "floor effect" likely renders the marginal benefit of exercise statistically undetectable over a short timeframe. In the cognition-impaired-only cohort (Cohort C), the neurodegenerative cascade may be too advanced. Once structural brain changes have manifested as clinical cognitive impairment, moderate physical activity may be insufficient to halt the secondary onset of depressive symptoms, which often manifest as behavioral and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD) [11].

Crucially, the depression-only phase (Cohort B) represents a reversible clinical inflection point. Late-life depression is increasingly recognized as a prodrome or independent risk factor for dementia [10]. Exercise is uniquely positioned to disrupt this specific pathological bridge. Mechanistically, physical activity upregulates Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF), enhances hippocampal neurogenesis, improves vascular endothelial function, and exerts anti-inflammatory effects [7, 8]. This supports exercise as a disease-modifying intervention, rather than a symptomatic one. Our causal estimates provide real-world evidence that prescribing exercise to older adults presenting with depressive symptoms can sever the neurobiological link between affective distress and subsequent cognitive collapse. For clinicians, this means that an initial diagnosis of late-life depression should trigger a structured exercise prescription as a primary preventative measure against impending cognitive decline.

### 4.2 The Paradox of Chronic Disease in Cognitive Decline
The finding that low chronic disease burden increases comorbidity risk in Cohort C highlights the complexities of geriatric epidemiology. This paradox can be explained through the lens of competing risks and the "frailty index." Older adults with cognitive impairment *and* high physical multimorbidity have a high short-term mortality risk; they may simply not survive long enough to develop incident depression (survival bias) [9]. Conversely, those with cognitive impairment but *no* physical diseases likely suffer from a primary, aggressive neurodegenerative disease (like Alzheimer's) rather than vascular dementia. This underscores the danger of interpreting standard regression coefficients as causal in advanced aging cohorts and emphasizes the need for stage-specific clinical expectations.

### 4.3 Methodological Innovations
This study advances the field methodologically by bridging predictive ML with causal inference. Models achieved moderate to strong discrimination, consistent with real-world multimorbidity risk prediction in aging populations. Traditional predictive models are excellent at identifying *who* is at risk, but they cannot answer *what to do* about it. By feeding our rigorously defined cohorts into the XLearner algorithm, we isolated the causal impact of specific variables. 

The robustness of our findings is underscored by the E-value analysis (E-value = 2.0). In the context of social epidemiology, where extensive physical, psychological, and socioeconomic covariates are already controlled for, an E-value of 2.0 is considered highly robust. It is highly improbable that an unmeasured confounder exists with a magnitude strong enough to nullify the protective effect of exercise without being highly correlated with the covariates already in the model [6]. The consistency across XLearner, PSW, and PSM further cements the validity of the causal claim, providing a reliable foundation for evidence-based clinical guidelines.

### 4.4 Limitations
First, the observational design precludes definitive causal inference. Although our E-value suggests the effect is robust, residual confounding by unmeasured factors such as diet, physical function severity, or genetic predisposition cannot be fully ruled out. *Future RCTs are warranted to confirm the causal effect of exercise in the depression-only cohort.* Second, overlap trimming excluded **~50%** of Cohort B, so the ATE applies specifically to the sub-population with clinical equipoise regarding exercise (i.e., those who *could* realistically exercise but might not be). *Future studies with larger samples may reduce the trimming proportion and extend generalizability.* Third, Cohort C showed overlap and balance violations; causal estimates there should be interpreted with caution. Fourth, cognitive impairment was defined using a composite score derived from epidemiological screening tools rather than a formal clinical diagnosis. *Validation against clinical diagnostic criteria would strengthen the findings; screening tools are nonetheless widely used in population-based aging research.* Fifth, some methodological refinements (e.g., median and IQR for skewed baseline variables, pairwise cohort comparisons, ECE, Brier decomposition, 95% CIs for external validation metrics) were not implemented in the current pipeline and may be added in future work. Finally, generalizability to other populations requires caution.

### 4.5 Conclusions
The progression to depression-cognition comorbidity in older adults is not a monolithic process, but a set of distinct trajectories requiring targeted interventions. By employing causal machine learning, we demonstrated that regular exercise is a potent, causal intervention for preventing cognitive decline specifically in older adults suffering from depression. These findings advocate for a paradigm shift in geriatric care and public health policy: moving away from generic advice, and instead integrating predictive screening to identify the "depression-only" phenotype, followed by aggressive, targeted lifestyle prescriptions (e.g., "exercise as medicine") to halt the trajectory toward debilitating comorbidity.

## Supplementary Text S1. Data Preprocessing and Imputation Strategy

**Imputation variable set.** Outcome and defining variables (is_comorbidity_next, is_comorbidity, is_depression, is_cognitive_impairment, total_cognition, cesd10) were not imputed; missing rows were excluded. **Continuous covariates** imputed: age, BMI, waist, blood pressure, pulse, grip, gait speed, ADL, IADL, family size, income, sleep. **Categorical covariates** imputed: gender, chronic diseases (hypertension, diabetes, cancer, lung, heart, stroke, arthritis), residence, education, marital status, exercise, drinking, social isolation, pension, insurance, retirement, self-rated health, life satisfaction, fall history, disability.

**Imputation model types.** Continuous variables: method selected by lowest NRMSE among mean, median, linear regression, Bayesian ridge, KNN, MissForest (IterativeImputer with RandomForest); ordinal (education, self-rated health): Bayesian regression with rounding; binary (exercise, drinking, chronic diseases, etc.): random forest classifier; nominal: mode. Auxiliary variables (baseline_group, province, wave, age) were used as predictors but not imputed. Physical bounds were applied (e.g., BMI 10–60, age 60–120).

**Multiple imputation and Rubin’s rules.** After NRMSE-based selection of the best continuous-variable method, five imputed datasets were generated (N_MULTIPLE_IMPUTATIONS=5) using that method. For pooled estimates across imputations, Rubin’s rules were applied: point estimate = mean of *m* estimates; total variance = within-imputation variance + (1 + 1/*m*) × between-imputation variance. Primary AUC and ATE results use Rubin-pooled estimates; SHAP and interpretability figures use a single imputed dataset (step1_imputed_full.csv) for consistency.

**Leakage prevention (must match code).** **Bulk MICE** that produces the analysis dataset is run on the **full analytic cohort** (before the grouped train–test split), so test-set rows contribute information to imputed values—this is **not** fold-wise imputation. **Prediction models:** `Pipeline` preprocessors are fit **only on training folds** during CV; **CPM** optimal thresholds are determined without using the final test fold. Test-set **labels** are not used for imputation or threshold tuning.

## Supplementary Text S3. Multiple testing, approximate *P* values, and FDR-style adjustment

**Purpose.** Observational analyses estimated average treatment effects (ATEs) for five binary lifestyle factors within three baseline cohorts using **XLearner** as the primary estimator, with **PSM** and **PSW** reported for triangulation. This yields **many** numeric contrasts when all estimators and exposures are tabulated. We document how **exploratory multiplicity adjustments** are produced in the reproducible output without replacing the **primary** inferential approach in the main text (ATE, 95% CI, bootstrap *P* for pre-specified primary contrasts).

**Approximate two-sided *p* (*p*\_approx).** For each row of the exported summary table that contains an ATE and its **95% confidence interval** (lower bound LB, upper bound UB), we approximate a two-sided *p* under a normal latent scale: standard error SE = (UB − LB) / (2 × 1.96); *z* = |ATE| / SE; *p*\_approx = 2 × (1 − Φ(|*z*|)), where Φ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function. This is a **convenience approximation** when only ATE and CI are archived for a given row; it need not match a bootstrap *p* digit-for-digit.

**Bonferroni.** Let *m* be the number of rows with non-missing *p*\_approx in the exported table. We set *p*\_adj_Bonf = min(*p*\_approx × *m*, 1).

**FDR-style adjustment.** We also compute *p*\_adj\_fdr using a rank-based adjustment on *p*\_approx (implementation: `utils/multiplicity_correction.py`, function `apply_bonferroni_fdr`). **This is intended as a transparency aid**; it applies to **all rows** in that machine-readable table (including PSM and PSW rows when present), not only to a pre-specified family such as “5 exposures × 3 cohorts for XLearner only.”

**Primary vs. exploratory.** **Primary** claims rely on the **XLearner** estimate, its **95% CI**, and the **bootstrap *P*** in the main tables for prespecified interventions of interest (especially exercise in Cohort B). Columns *p*\_adj_Bonf and *p*\_adj\_fdr in the supplementary export are **secondary**; a finding can remain primary-significant by CI and bootstrap *P* even if *p*\_adj\_fdr exceeds 0.05 for a broad global table.

**Reproducibility.** The augmented table is written after a full run, e.g. `LIU_JUE_STRATEGIC_SUMMARY/xlearner_all_interventions/xlearner_all_interventions_summary.csv`. Columns include `p_value_approx`, `p_adj_bonferroni`, `p_adj_fdr`, and flags `significant_95`, `significant_bonferroni`, `significant_fdr` where applicable.
