We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled "**Development and validation of a prediction model for incident depression–cognition comorbidity in older adults in China: a CHARLS-based study with exploration of potential intervention effects**" for consideration in *[Journal Name]*.

Depression and cognitive impairment frequently co-occur in older adults, leading to accelerated functional decline and mortality. However, existing research often treats older adults as a homogeneous group, failing to distinguish whether comorbidity arises from a healthy baseline, progresses from depression, or evolves from cognitive decline. This lack of stratification limits the precision of preventive interventions.

**Our study addresses this critical gap by proposing a novel "Three-Cohort Causal Machine Learning Framework".** Using longitudinal data from 14,386 participants in the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), we stratified older adults into three distinct baseline cohorts: Healthy, Depression-only, and Cognition-impaired-only.

**Key highlights of our findings include:**
1.  **Heterogeneity of Disease Progression:** We identified distinct incidence rates and risk profiles across the three cohorts, validating the necessity of a stratified approach.
2.  **Causal Evidence for Exercise:** Using state-of-the-art **Causal Forest Double Machine Learning (DML)**, validated by Propensity Score Weighting (PSW), we found a consistent protective trend for physical exercise in preventing comorbidity among older adults with pre-existing depression (Cohort B).
3.  **Insight into Reverse Causality:** In contrast, for those with pre-existing cognitive impairment (Cohort C), our causal analysis revealed a counter-intuitive positive association, which we rigorously interpret through the lens of survivorship bias and reverse causality, offering a nuanced perspective often missed in traditional regression studies.
4.  **Clinical Utility:** Our Decision Curve Analysis (DCA) demonstrates that the developed prediction models offer substantial net benefit for clinical screening.

We believe this work makes a significant methodological and clinical contribution to the field of geriatric mental health and precision medicine. It moves beyond simple association to provide actionable, causal evidence for targeted interventions.
